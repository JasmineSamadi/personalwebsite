Quantitative Research Analyst

As a final project for Cornell's Extreme Values in Finance class, I conducted quantitative research analyzing historical data on Novo Nordisk and NVIDIA stocks, sourced from Yahoo Finance.

Using R, I developed code to assess tail behavior and estimate shape parameters through mean excess plots and estimators such as Hills, Pickands, and Moment. The findings revealed that both stocks exhibit heavy-tailed distributions, fitting a Frechet distribution. I also calculated the extremal index to analyze the clustering of extreme values, identifying that a significant portion of extreme data occurs in clusters for both stocks.

The research highlighted similar clustering behaviors between the two stocks and provided insights into their tail risk.

ORIE Data Analyst

This project analyzed the relationship between the Gender Inequality Index (GII) and social and economic factors, leveraging Python for statistical modeling and data visualization. GII is a composite number developed by the United Nations indicating gender disparity, where a GII of 0 indicates perfect gender equality and 1 indicates maximum gender inequality.

We examined over 190 countries and explored factors including adolescent birth rates, secondary education rates, and UN policies aimed at increasing women's representation in decision-making. A Python-based predictive model with a train-test split methodology was implemented to analyze time series data, providing insights into trends and correlations.

Logistic regression demonstrated that GII is a significant predictor for classifying a country's development status. Regression analysis revealed that GII has a strong, negative impact on male secondary education attainment and a positive correlation with adolescent birth rates. Policies like UN Resolution 2242 were analyzed through time-series modeling, by predicting GII data as if these policies were not put into place and comparing them with actual data.

Simulation Modeling Analyst

This project explores biases in hiring processes that lead to gender disparity in the workplace, specifically the favoring of male candidates, by simulating hiring and promotion practices.

We model gender, seniority, homophily bias, and education level as key factors affecting hiring and promotion decisions, investigating their independent and combined effects. The simulation uses a Markov chain model to track gender distribution across corporate levels, with factors such as promotion bias and departure rates influencing outcomes.

Our findings suggest that initial gender imbalance, especially at senior levels, exacerbates gender disparities over time, and that education can help reduce these d isparities even in the presence of homophily bias. The model highlights the importance of addressing both gender bias in hiring and the retention of women in senior positions to improve overall workplace diversity. Based on the results, we recommend promoting merit-based evaluations and focusing on reducing departure rates to foster a more equitable work environment.
